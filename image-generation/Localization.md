## **Cultural Localization Add-on:** 

---

Here's a universal add-on you can append to any "translate this image into [language]" instruction:

```

Do not translate literally — **localize**. Your goal is that a native [target language] reader/viewer feels the exact same emotion, social tension, humor, or subtext that a native [source language] reader/viewer would feel — even if that means the words, idioms, or specific references change completely.

 Before writing the final text, think through:
 1. **Intent & Emotion**: What is the speaker actually *doing* socially (mocking, flirting, complaining, guilt-tripping, joking)? What emotion should the reader feel — irritation, awkwardness, smugness, relief?
 2. **Cultural Equivalence**: Does this reference, norm, stereotype, or joke exist in the target culture? If not, replace it with a local equivalent that carries the same social weight (e.g., a Japanese-specific dating/dining norm should become whatever the target culture's closest comparable norm or stereotype is — not a word-for-word rendering).
 3. **Register & Voice**: Match tone, formality, age, and personality of the speaker naturally in the target language (casual, sarcastic, blunt, sweet, etc.) — not textbook-correct phrasing.
 4. **Natural Phrasing**: Write like a native speaker actually talks/writes in this medium (comic, meme, sign, subtitle, ad), not like a dictionary. Avoid stiff, overly literal, or "translated" cadence.
 5. **Fit**: Keep line/text length appropriate for the original text bubble, caption box, or space — don't let localized phrasing overflow.
 
> Everything else in the image — art, characters, layout, panel composition — must remain **completely unchanged**. Only replace the embedded text.

```

**How to use it:**
Just paste it right after your instruction, e.g.:

"Translate this image into English. [paste add-on above]"
